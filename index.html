Students weren't engaging with TechRise not because they didn't need it but because the app made the wrong things hard. I led the redesign from research through shipped UI.
TechRise
redesigning a student productivity app to actually get used
Timeline
4 weeks
My Role
UI/UX Designer (solo)
Collaborators
Francis Ganya (engineer)
Deliverables
Research synthesis, user flows, wireframes, final UI, advisor variant
01context
the app existed. the problem was adoption.
TechRise is a student-focused platform with two core jobs: help students book advisor appointments and surface relevant internship opportunities. the product had real utility on paper. but engagement was low, and the team had a hunch the interface was the reason.

i came in to diagnose whether that was true and if so, where exactly the experience was breaking down.
02problem framing
what we were actually solving for
before touching anything, i needed to separate the symptoms from the real problem. the app being "cluttered" was a description, not a problem statement.

students aren't engaging with TechRise because the cost of navigating the app exceeds the perceived value of what they'll find when they get there, especially for time-sensitive actions like booking appointments or finding internship deadlines.
this reframe mattered because it shifted the design goal. the question wasn't "how do we clean this up?" it was "how do we make the highest-value actions feel effortless and fast?"
03research
what students actually said
i ran user interviews and distributed a survey to understand where the friction was coming from. i also did a competitive teardown of advisor booking and internship tools to understand what the bar looked like.

user interviews: recurring quotes:
  • "it's hard to find advisors that have time to help me."
  • "by the time i find the posting, it's too late to apply."
  • "switching between different platforms for internship postings gets frustrating."

survey data:
60%
struggle to book appointments
90%
want internships easier to access
50%
find the interface confusing
30%
want meeting reminders

competitive analysis takeaways:
  • existing advisor booking tools are functional but built for admin convenience, not student speed
  • Handshake has volume but weak filtering; students waste time before finding relevant postings
  • common theme: students value speed and clarity over feature richness
04synthesis & prioritization
turning findings into a focused scope
ProblemSeverityIn Scope?Rationale
Confusing navigation / IAHigh✓ YesRoot cause of most friction
Advisor availability not visibleHigh✓ YesDirectly impacts core use case
Internship deadline visibilityHigh✓ YesZero-cost fix via info hierarchy
Notification systemMediumFutureBackend dependency
Cross-platform aggregationHigh (complex)FutureOut of sprint feasibility
the call to scope out notifications and integrations was deliberate. trying to solve everything in 4 weeks would have compromised the structural redesign.
05design decisions
the choices i made and why
i mapped the three core user flows before touching UI: booking an advisor, finding and applying to an internship, and tracking active tasks/deadlines.
  • restructured navigation hierarchy: reduced top-level items; moved highest-frequency actions to persistent nav.
  • advisor availability surfaced on browse: open slots visible in directory view; reduces booking from 4+ taps to 2.
  • deadline prominence on internship cards: deadlines moved from detail page to card-level summary.
  • task tracking simplified: categorization reduced to three buckets (to-do, in progress, done).
06tradeoffs
what i gave up to get here
  • simplified task states vs. power user flexibility: students who wanted granular project tracking lost some customization. i accepted this because the majority were getting stuck on complexity.
  • no onboarding flow redesign: first-run experience had friction but was a week of work alone. documented and flagged for a follow-up sprint.
  • advisor UI as a variant, not a separate product: advisor-facing screens share the same component logic with the student view; kept engineering scope tight while unlocking a future use case.
07output
what shipped
  • redesigned navigation and information architecture across student-facing screens
  • new advisor booking flow: reduced steps, availability visible upfront
  • internship board with deadline-forward card design
  • simplified task tracker with three-state categorization
  • advisor-facing UI variant for future engineering use
designs were handed off to Francis Ganya and implemented in the live TechRise app.
08reflection
what i'd do differently
  • validate the reframe earlier: i reframed the problem based on my read of the research but never explicitly tested that framing with stakeholders before designing against it.
  • measure the thing i was trying to move: the brief didn't define a success metric. establishing a north star (e.g. task completion rate or time-to-book) would have sharpened every decision.
  • document scope decisions as artifacts: i made deliberate calls to descope notifications and integrations, but those decisions lived in my head. capturing them as a scope doc would have made handoff cleaner.
Trace
building a brand identity from scratch before launch
Timeline
1 week
My Role
CMO
Collaborators
Arhaan Keshwani, Joey Zhang, Nina Kilidzhiyska
Deliverables
Logo, typeface, product display format, launch assets
01context
the reminder app you can talk to
trace is a conversational reminders iOS app. users talk to it naturally, and it handles the structure. i'm the cmo, working alongside arhaan keshwani, joey zhang, and nina kilidzhiyska.

we had a product but no visual language. no logo, no typeface, no defined way to show the app to people. with launch approaching, we needed brand decisions that could scale across social posts, product displays, and app store assets, made fast, with no dedicated design budget.

3
brand pillars to lock in
1wk
total
$0
design budget
02the problem
without a shared visual identity, every piece of content we produced would read as disconnected.
more practically: i couldn't make product display graphics, social posts, or launch assets without knowing what trace actually looks like.

the three decisions blocking everything downstream were logo, typeface, and how to show the product in marketing. until those were locked, we couldn't build anything repeatable.

as the sole designer on the team, i was responsible for driving each brand decision to a conclusion while keeping the rest of the team aligned through async gc feedback.
03decision 01: logo
at this point we had nothing locked. no wordmark, no typeface, no visual direction.
the only alignment we had was that we wanted the brand to feel simplistic. the first logo attempt was a glowing abstract waveform mark: white on black, soft-edged, radiating outward. it looked striking in isolation but immediately read as too complex and too heavy for where we wanted to go. it didn't feel like trace.

first logo attempt: glowing waveform mark

that reaction was useful. it told us what we didn't want. from there, the team moved toward a geometric mark, but i pushed back: without a brand identity established yet, something that abstract gave no signal about what trace actually did. i suggested the voice memo waveform direction and put together multiple variations to show what that could look like. we tested those with users and got positive feedback. but as the brand developed, the font and product displays started doing the heavy lifting on communication, and we grew more confident that the logo didn't need to spell out the product. combined with the original instinct toward something minimal and clean, we came back to the geometric mark: simple enough to sit small without losing its shape, and flexible enough to grow with the brand.

second logo attempt: hexagon mark

suggestions after second attempt: voice memo waveform iterations

optionrationaleverdict
glowing waveform markvisually interesting but too ornate. the soft glow and radiating shapes contradicted the simplistic direction we had aligned oncut
abstract hexagon markgeometric, clean, distinctive. not tied to any single product feature, which gave trace room to build its own identity around the mark over time. once we had a strong font and product displays carrying the communication, the logo could just be a markchosen
voice memo style icon (waveform bars)cleaner than v1, referenced the app's core voice interaction directly. explored multiple iterations. ultimately felt too literal and too tied to a single featurecut

final thoughts: the voice memo iterations were a necessary middle step. they were cleaner than the v1 and pointed in the right direction, but the more the rest of the brand system came together (typeface, product displays), the less the logo needed to explain what trace does. that freed us to go fully abstract. the hexagon mark tested well with the team and gave the brand more flexibility long-term.
04decision 02: typeface
the font conversation started because the assets i was building felt off.
the team's instinct was to go fully minimalist, which had a clear vision behind it. but from a consumer standpoint, minimalism alone wasn't going to sell the product. i pushed for something with more personality, while making clear it didn't need to go overboard. there was a middle ground and we needed to find it.

we anchored off the font already on the trace website (a clean sans used for body), then focused the search on a display face that could carry the wordmark and headers.

first font suggestion: building point

satoshi medium: not enough character
satoshi black italic: too heavy
coolvetica: too elementary
apollo otf: the middle ground

with apollo we tested both cases. uppercase read too formal. lowercase felt intentional and fit the conversational nature of the product. we locked in all lowercase as the wordmark.

final font: apollo otf
05decision 03: product display format
the product display went through two distinct phases: before and after the rebrand.
phase 1: pre-rebrand
the first attempts used a "hey trace" headline format with a single angled phone mockup on a light starburst background. the copy and layout were bold and attention-grabbing but the visual didn't feel like the product. it leaned too editorial, and without the brand system finalized, nothing was anchoring it to trace specifically.

phase 2: post-rebrand iterations
once the font and logo were locked, i rebuilt the product display around the finalized brand. the format shifted to a dark background with three angled phone mockups showing the app in context. this immediately felt more like trace. the team responded positively but it still took a few passes to get the hierarchy right.

phase 2b: incorporating feedback
after the initial iterations, two clear pieces of feedback emerged: the visual hierarchy needed work and the logo was missing entirely. the wordmark was either too large and competing with the phones, or absent altogether, which left the display feeling unanchored from the brand. taking that feedback into account, i brought the sizing down, repositioned the elements, and reintroduced the logo in a way that complemented the product shots rather than overpowering them.

06outcome
a working brand system, locked and documented.
by the end of these three decision threads we had a working brand system: a hexagon icon mark, the apollo otf wordmark in lowercase, and a product display format we could repeat across launch assets.

the hexagon mark and apollo typeface ended up complementing each other in a way that gave trace a clear identity and tone. the geometric precision of the mark paired with the editorial character of the font reads as minimal but intentional, which is exactly the middle ground we were after. together they establish what trace looks and feels like before a single feature gets explained.

more importantly, we had a documented visual language the whole team understood. anything we produced after this (social posts, the app store listing, paid ads) could be built on the same foundation without re-litigating the same choices.

07reflections
what i'd do differently
  • decisions need a framing, not just a vote: the logo and font conversations moved faster once i framed them around what trace needs to communicate to a stranger, not which option looks better in isolation. "does this tell someone what the product is" became the filter.
  • pushing back on your own team is part of the job: i disagreed on coolvetica being too corporate and on the flat screenshot format not doing enough work. both pushbacks led to better outcomes. as cmo, part of the role is holding the brand standard even when something "looks fine."
  • async gc feedback is fast but incomplete: we used the gc as a lightweight review layer throughout. it moved quickly, but "tested well with some people" isn't a substitute for structured user feedback. for a v2 brand refresh, i'd want actual target users reacting to options, not just team consensus.
  • establish a shared brand brief earlier: even a half-page doc covering tone, audience, and visual references, before jumping into asset iterations. we made good decisions but arrived at them through back-and-forth rather than from a shared north star.
